# Evaluating the Global Consensus on Keratoconus and Ectatic Diseases Agreements Reached on Subclinical Keratoconus (Randleman et al. 2025)

> A literature review of 29 studies evaluating the diagnostic performance of anterior corneal surface, thickness, and posterior corneal surface metrics for **subclinical keratoconus (SKC)** detection. The headline result directly contradicts the 2015 Global Consensus on Keratoconus and Ectatic Diseases statement that *"posterior corneal elevation abnormalities must be present to diagnose mild or subclinical keratoconus."* **Anterior surface metrics led in 37.9% of intrastudy comparisons, thickness metrics in 39.2%, and posterior surface metrics in only 13.8%.** Across the entire 2011–2022 timeframe, no four-year interval showed posterior metrics outperforming anterior or thickness metrics. *American Journal of Ophthalmology* 275:27–35, July 2025. NIH-funded (R01EY028666, R01EY032537, R01EY028667, R01EY032633) + Research to Prevent Blindness departmental grant. CC BY-NC-ND 4.0 open access.

## Summary

The 2015 Global Consensus on Keratoconus and Ectatic Diseases (Gomes et al., *Cornea* 34:359–369) was a modified-Delphi consensus across four supranational corneal societies — **Asia Cornea Society, Cornea Society, EuCornea, and Pan-Cornea**. Three panels were formed; one was specifically tasked with keratoconus definition and diagnosis. The panel reached consensus agreements including:

1. *"Abnormal posterior elevation and abnormal corneal thickness distribution are mandatory to diagnose keratoconus."*
2. *"Posterior corneal elevation abnormalities must be present to diagnose mild or subclinical keratoconus."*

The 2015 consensus paper did not provide primary data, primary references, or evidence to support these claims. Randleman et al. note that the consensus "was met with significant controversy" upon publication (Saad & Gatinel 2015 *Cornea* 34:e33–e34) and that no subsequent paper has produced supporting evidence. This 2025 paper systematically reviews the post-2015 literature to evaluate whether the consensus claim about posterior elevation in SKC is supported.

**Method.** PubMed search January 2, 2024 across terms *posterior corneal elevation, keratoconus screening, corneal ectasia, subclinical keratoconus, keratoconus suspect, asymmetric keratoconus*. 1,245 records → title screening (848 excluded) → abstract screening (185 excluded) → full text (212 assessed, 183 excluded) → **29 articles included for final analysis**. Cross-referenced with the Moshirfar 2019 *Med Hypothesis Discov Innov Ophthalmol* two-part Pentacam-screening review series and the 2024 AAO Ophthalmic Technology Assessment paper on advanced corneal imaging.

**Inclusion criteria.** Studies must (1) include a group classifiable as "subclinical keratoconus" — though nomenclature varies (forme fruste keratoconus, keratoconus suspect, asymmetric keratoconus, subclinical keratoconus); (2) evaluate posterior corneal surface metrics with comparison to anterior surface and/or thickness metrics; (3) report AUROC for multiple variables. AUROC differences within 0.03 considered equivalent unless > 5% difference in sensitivity.

**Three metric subgroups.** Anterior (A), Thickness (T), Posterior (P). Plus the **multimetric Pentacam D score** (Oculus) evaluated as a separate metric since it combines anterior + thickness + posterior + biomechanical inputs.

## Key claims and findings

### Headline cross-study performance (Table 4)

| Subgroup | n | Anterior best | Thickness best | Posterior best | D score best | Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Overall (all 29 papers) | 29 | **11 (37.9%)** | **11 (39.2%)** | 4 (13.8%) | — | 3 (10.3%) |
| D-score subgroup | 15 | 5 (33.3%) | 2 (3.3%) | 0 (0%) | 4 (26.7%) | 4 (26.7%) |

Only **4/29 papers (13.8%)** reported posterior metrics outperforming all other metrics. **In the D-score subgroup, no paper reported superior posterior-metric performance.**

### Temporal trend (Figure 2) — no era of posterior superiority

Across 2011–2014, 2015–2018, and 2019–2022 four-year intervals, **anterior surface and thickness metrics outperformed posterior surface metrics in every interval.** The historical claim that posterior elevation is the "earliest sign" of forme fruste keratoconus (Rao et al. 2002 *Ophthalmology* 109:1642–1646) is not supported by any subsequent temporal window of evidence.

### The D-score performs well but not best on anterior comparison

The **D score** (final Belin/Ambrósio Enhanced Ectasia Display from Pentacam, Oculus) is a multimetric classifier combining anterior surface, thickness, biomechanical, and posterior elevation inputs. It performed best in 26.7% of the D-subgroup papers, **but anterior surface metrics still led at 33.3%**. The D score never outperformed the final D score (tautological) but also never outperformed anterior-surface metrics consistently when both were available — suggesting the multimetric advantage is modest relative to well-chosen anterior metrics.

### KISA% — explicit deprecation recommendation

The Rabinowitz KISA% index (Rabinowitz & Rasheed 1999 *J Cataract Refract Surg* 25:1327–1335) appears in 7/10 (70%) papers among those using the D-score, as a secondary classification criterion (typically KISA% < 60 used to exclude clinical keratoconus from the SKC cohort). The 2025 review **explicitly recommends against using KISA% for SKC classification**:

> "Numerous articles have highlighted the lack of sensitivity of the KISA% index to identify manifest keratoconus and have demonstrated that neither KISA% index <60 in isolation nor combining KISA% <60 with IS value <1.4 excludes clinical keratoconus cases from being classified as subclinical. Since this classification system cannot exclude clinical keratoconic disease as being classified as normal topography, we do not recommend its use for subclinical disease classification."

Primary support citation: Hammoud, Dupps Jr, Scarcelli, Randleman 2024 *J Refract Surg* 40:e614–e24, *"Misclassification of eyes with progressive keratoconus using the KISA% index."*

### Subclinical keratoconus nomenclature variability — a primary obstacle

The reviewed papers used inconsistent definitions of "subclinical keratoconus." Many classification systems would not exclude clinical-keratoconus cases. Only ~1 in 5 papers (6 of 29) used corrected distance visual acuity (CDVA) of 20/20 or better as an inclusion criterion. Only 5/29 (12.8%) provided corneal imaging for at least most of their subclinical cohort, limiting independent reclassification. The authors recommend:

- Reduced best corrected acuity should disqualify an eye from "subclinical" classification.
- CDVA ≥ 20/20 should be a standard inclusion criterion.
- Standardized clinical-grade definitions of subclinical keratoconus need to be established.

This is a primary-source finding that **the basic clinical entity "subclinical keratoconus" lacks a standard operationalization in the literature**. AI/ML screening tools trained on heterogeneously labeled datasets across the field face an unresolved label-quality problem.

### Posterior elevation as biomechanical proxy — historical positioning

The discussion section traces the 2015 consensus's posterior-elevation claim to:

- **Tomidokoro et al. 2000** *Ophthalmology* 107:1328–1332 — first reported changes in both anterior and posterior curvatures in keratoconus suspects.
- **Rao et al. 2002** *Ophthalmology* 109:1642–1646 — proposed posterior elevation as the *"earliest sign of FFKC"*, but acknowledged *"there is no conclusive direct evidence that patients with high posterior elevation are at increased risk of ectasia."*
- **de Sanctis et al. 2008** *Ophthalmology* 115:1534–1539 — first formal AUROC evaluation: posterior elevation worked for clinically manifest KC, but discrimination was *"significantly worse in eyes with subclinical KC."* The authors themselves concluded that *"indices derived from Placido disk-based videokeratography may be more sensitive in discriminating this condition."*

The 2015 consensus statement was therefore not supported by the most directly relevant prior empirical work — including the work the consensus appears to descend from.

### Cited AI/ML keratoconus papers in the review

## Why this paper matters

For this wiki:

1. **Guideline-vs-evidence drift documented.** The 2015 Global Consensus on Keratoconus statement was issued by Delphi panel without primary data. Ten years later, a NIH-funded literature review finds the central claim is unsupported by the published evidence. This is a clean primary-source case study of guideline-consensus diverging from empirical performance — relevant to [clinical-validation](../concepts/clinical-validation.md) framing on how clinical AI should be grounded in primary evidence rather than expert consensus.
2. **Current state of engineered-metric performance for SKC screening (2025).** The wiki's [keratoconus-screening-ai](../concepts/keratoconus-screening-ai.md) lineage tracks engineered indices from 1994 onward. This paper is the most current systematic evaluation of which categories of those indices actually work best for SKC detection: **anterior surface ≈ thickness > posterior, with the multimetric D score doing well but not consistently best**. The KPI/DSI/OSI/CSI/SAI/IAI indices from the [maeda-klyce-keratoconus-screening-1994](maeda-klyce-keratoconus-screening-1994.md) paper are anterior-surface metrics — consistent with this 2025 evidence ordering.
3. **Direct deprecation of KISA%.** Combined with primary-source citation (Hammoud et al. 2024), this paper recommends against using the Rabinowitz KISA% index for SKC classification. The [keratoconus-screening-ai](../concepts/keratoconus-screening-ai.md) page already mentioned KISA% briefly — this update establishes the 2024–25 consensus position that KISA% is inadequate for the subclinical task.
4. **Label-quality crisis for the SKC task.** Across 29 papers, "subclinical keratoconus" has no standard definition. This is a foundational data-quality problem for any AI/ML training on heterogeneous SKC datasets — the same labels mean different clinical entities across studies. Modern deep-learning keratoconus systems trained against published SKC labels inherit this label drift, which the wiki's deep-learning rung-4 coverage should reflect.
5. **Author cluster confirms refractive-surgery authority.** Randleman is the bibliometrician whose 2023 paper ranks [Klyce as #11 most-cited author in Refractive Surgery](../entities/stephen-klyce.md). Koch (Baylor) and Dupps Jr (Cleveland Clinic) are senior refractive-surgery PIs. The author cluster — combined with the NIH R01 funding (4 separate R01s acknowledged) — indicates this is a high-authority primary source for current refractive-surgery field positions.
